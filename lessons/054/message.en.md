🇪🇸 *Lesson 54*

You just unlocked hundreds of new Spanish adjectives\. And you've been using this pattern since lesson one without knowing it\.

Here's the trick: those verb forms that come after *haber* \(like in "I have closed it" — *lo he cerrado*\) can work as adjectives\. Just like in English, where "sold" works in both "I have sold" and "it is sold\."

— Where's Miguel?
— *Está perdido\.* He got lost on the way\.
— And the restaurant?
— *Está cerrado\.* Closed on Mondays\.

The pattern works like this:
After *haber* → always ONE form
As an adjective → it changes to match the noun

*Eight you'll use constantly:*
_cerrado_ — closed
_perdido_ — lost
_terminado_ — finished
_vendido_ — sold
_cansado_ — tired
_mojado_ — wet
_roto_ — broken
_preparado_ — ready

💡 *These adjectives almost always use ESTAR\.* Why? Because they describe states, not permanent characteristics\. *Está cerrado* \(it's closed\), *estoy cansado* \(I'm tired\)\. If you say "it IS a \[adjective\] \[noun\]" — like "it's a closed person" — use SER: *Es una persona cerrada\.* You're naming what someone IS, not describing a temporary state\.

👉 *Try this:* Pick three adjectives from the list\. Say them out loud with *estoy* \(I am\) and *está* \(it is/he is/she is\)\.
