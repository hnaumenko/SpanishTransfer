🇪🇸 *Lesson 55: The "Should Have" Trick \(That Uses Words You Already Know\)*

You know how we say "I have cancelled" in English? In Spanish, you say _he cancelado_\. But here's the twist: that *cancelado* part? You can use it as an adjective too\.

— How's the party going?
— _Está cancelada\._ \(It's cancelled\.\)

See that? Same word, different job\. And this unlocks something huge: *should have* and *could have*\.

To say "I should," you use _debería_ \(would owe\)\. To say "I could," you use _podría_ \(would be able\)\. Now add _haber_ \(to have\) \+ that adjective form:

_Debería haber cancelado_ — I should have cancelled
_Podría haber ido_ — I could have gone

*8 examples you'll actually use:*

_Está complicado_ — It's complicated
_Está roto_ — It's broken
_Ha muerto_ — He has died
_Estoy muerto_ — I'm dead \(exhausted\)
_Está abierto_ — It's open
_Lo he dicho_ — I have said it
_Está hecho_ — It's done
_Hecho en China_ — Made in China

💡 Notice: _muerto, roto, abierto, hecho_ \(dead, broken, open, done\) are irregular\. They don't follow the \-ado/\-ido pattern\. But they still work the same way\.

👉 *Say this now:* _Debería haberlo hecho_ \(I should have done it\)\.
