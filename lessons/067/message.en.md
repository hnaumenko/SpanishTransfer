🇪🇸 *Lesson 67: How Stress Marks Work in Spanish*

You already know where to stress Spanish words\. You just don't realize it yet\.

Here's the secret: Spanish has only *two natural rules* for stress\. Break them, and you get an accent mark\. Follow them, and the word stays clean\.

*Rule 1:* If a word ends in a vowel, N, or S → stress the *second\-to\-last* syllable
*Rule 2:* If it ends in *any other consonant* → stress the *last* syllable

Why vowels, N, and S? Because that's what verbs end in\. Spanish built the whole system around them\.

— _¿Hablas español?_
— _Sí, hablo un poco\._

No accent marks\. Why? They end in S and O, so the stress naturally falls on the second\-to\-last syllable: *ha\-BLAS*, *ha\-BLO*\.

But infinitives? They end in R:
_comer, estar, hablar, encontrar_

Stress the end: co\-MER, es\-TAR\. No accent mark needed—they're following Rule 2\.

*Now here's where it gets interesting:*

When a word *breaks* the rule, you slap on an accent mark to show it\.

_tradición_ — tradition
_encontró_ — he/she/you found
_vivió_ — he/she/you lived
_limón_ — lemon
_está_ — he/she/you is/are
_opinión_ — opinion
_canción_ — song
_café_ — coffee

Each one ends in N, O, or E, so you'd *expect* the stress on the second\-to\-last syllable\. But it's not\. The accent mark tells you: "Stress *this* syllable instead\."

💡 *Quick trick:* No accent mark? Use the two rules\. Accent mark? Stress that vowel\.

👉 *Try reading these out loud:* _hablan, hablaron, tradición, salud, café, general_
