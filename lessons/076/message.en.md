🇪🇸 *Lesson 76: The Word That Doesn't Change \(When English Does\)*

Here's something weird: English says "there *is*" and "there *are*" — the verb changes\. Spanish? Just one word for both: *hay*\.

Think about it:
— How many people are here?
— *Hay* una persona\. \(There *is* one person\.\)
— *Hay* diez personas\. \(There *are* ten people\.\)

Same word\. Always\. No change\.

And in the past? English says "there *was*" and "there *were*"\. Spanish uses *había* for both\. Never changes\.

*Here are 8 examples you'll use constantly:*

_Hay alguien aquí_ — There's someone here
_Hay dos personas_ — There are two people
_Había un problema_ — There was a problem
_Había muchos problemas_ — There were many problems
_Hay algo que puedes hacer_ — There's something you can do
_Hay tiempo_ — There's time
_Había comida_ — There was food
_Había tres opciones_ — There were three options

💡 *One trick:* When you say "there has to be" or "there must be," you're still using this invisible *hay* verb\. So it's *tiene que haber*, not *tiene que ser*\. 

*There has to be something* → _Tiene que haber algo_
