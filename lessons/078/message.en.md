🇪🇸 *Lesson 78: The Secret Patterns Hidden After Common Verbs*

Ever notice how some Spanish verbs are picky about what comes next? They demand a little word in between — and if you skip it, you'll sound… off\.

Here's what I mean: You can't just say *"empiezo hablar"*\. You need *"empiezo **a** hablar"* — "I start **to** speak\."

But here's where it gets interesting: after *empezar* \(to start\) and *terminar* \(to finish\), you can ALSO use the \-ing form — and the meaning shifts slightly\.

*Empiezo a hablar* — I start to speak
*Empiezo hablando* — I start out speaking \(right from the beginning\)

*Termino de vender* — I finish selling
*Termino vendiendo* — I end up selling

See the difference? The \-ing form adds this sense of "ending up" or "starting out\."

Now, check out these 8 examples:

_Empiezo a trabajar_ — I start to work
_Empiezo trabajando_ — I start out working
_Terminan de comer_ — They finish eating
_Terminan comiendo_ — They end up eating
_Comienza a llover_ — It starts to rain
_Es espectacular que empiecen a hablar español_ — It's spectacular that they start to speak Spanish
_Quiero que me hables cuando termines de venderlos_ — I want you to talk to me when you finish selling them
_Termino estudiando toda la noche_ — I end up studying all night

💡 If there's a preposition before the infinitive, watch for the \-ing alternative with a shift in meaning\.

👉 Try saying it out loud: *"Empiezo a entender\."* Now: *"Termino entendiendo\."*
