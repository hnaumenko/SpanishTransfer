🇪🇸 *Lesson 84: The "Go" Verbs That Break All the Rules \(But in a Predictable Way\)*

You've probably noticed some Spanish verbs act weird in the "I" form\. They don't follow the normal pattern\. Instead of ending in *\-o*, they end in *\-go*\. 

Here's the good news: they're *predictably* weird\. Once you know the pattern, you've unlocked a whole family of verbs\.

Look at this:
— *Tengo* un problema — I have a problem
— *Vengo* mañana — I'm coming tomorrow
— *Salgo* a las ocho* — I'm leaving at eight
— *Pongo* la mesa — I'm setting the table

See the *\-go*? That's your signal\. And here's the twist: when these verbs go into the mood tense \(the "I want that you\.\.\." tense\), the *\-go* becomes *\-g\-* for *everyone*\.

So *vengo* \(I come\) becomes *venga* \(that you come, that he comes, etc\.\)

*— Quiero que vengas mañana\.*
— I want you to come tomorrow\.

Here are 8 verbs that follow this rule:

_Tengo_ — I have
_Vengo_ — I come
_Salgo_ — I go out / leave
_Pongo_ — I put
_Hago_ — I do / make
_Oigo_ — I hear
_Traigo_ — I bring
_Digo_ — I say / tell

💡 When you answer the phone in Spain, you might hear *"Dime\!"* \(Tell me\!\)\. That's the mood tense as a command\.

👉 Try it: say *"No quiero que traigas eso"* \(I don't want you to bring that\)\.
